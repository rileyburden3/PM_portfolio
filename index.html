Cosmic Explorer

The artist formerly known as Cosmic Scale Explorer

Live

The problem: I wanted to deeply understand what building with Claude actually entails. So I built an interactive web app that lets anyone compare over 100 celestial objects at true to scale proportions, with AI generated fun facts for every pairing. Personal curiosity plus my 6 year old's interest in space equals a real product.

✨ See Live Demo
What I Built
  • 140+ space objects across multiple categories (asteroids, comets, planets, stars, exoplanets, black holes, and moons)
  • True scale visual comparisons with step ladder for extreme size differences
  • Moon Explorer tab: dedicated interface for exploring major moons of planets and dwarf planets, organized by parent body
  • Browsable grid with filters, sorting controls (by size, by orbital order), and live search
  • AI powered fun facts via Claude API (Claude Haiku) for every object
  • Tab-based navigation (Compare, Browse, Moon Explorer) with consistent visual rendering across all tabs
  • Shareable URLs so anyone can send a comparison directly
  • Audio pronunciation for each object
How I Built It and What Surprised Me

I directed Claude end to end: defining scope, making architecture decisions, iterating on UX. Claude handled all code. What surprised me most was how collaborative and intelligent the process was. Not just code output, but scaffolding the entire product (GitHub repo, Vercel deployment, API security decisions). It felt like working with an engineer, except instantaneous and without competing priorities. What would have taken me a month took hours.

For v2 specifically, the real lesson was iteration velocity. I didn't need to architect a complex solution for moons. I could polish and refine in real time. When I realized the single page interface was cramped, I pivoted to tabs. When I noticed color inconsistencies across tabs, I fixed them instantly. When sorting felt arbitrary, I standardized it. Each of these decisions would have been a day-long design process in traditional workflows. With Claude, they were minutes.

The Iteration (v1 to v2)

v1 foundation: I shipped v1 with 70+ celestial objects, true scale comparisons, and AI generated fun facts. My son used it regularly and loved it. But as he continued exploring, he started asking questions about moons: which planets have them, what are their names, how big are they? I realized he wanted deeper moon specific learning, and the single page interface wasn't set up for that. Everything scrolled in one long page, making it hard to navigate focused topics.

v2 decision: Rather than wait for him to complain, I proactively decided to expand the experience. I added a dedicated Moon Explorer tab with major moons for each planet and dwarf planet, and reorganized the entire interface into clear sections: Compare tab, Browse tab, Moon Explorer tab. The constraint of consistency forced intentional design. If I was adding tabs, every tab needed the same visual language, the same sorting logic, the same clarity. I standardized how celestial objects render across all views. I added sorting controls (by size, by orbital order). I grouped moons by parent planet in a logical sequence.

The rebuild: The whole thing took about a week (mixed with other work), and the architecture didn't need to change. This was pure UX and feature work. What surprised me: the constraint of consistency actually made the product stronger. I wasn't just adding a feature; I was elevating the entire experience. And the velocity was remarkable. I could iterate on colors, sorting, layouts, and visual consistency in real time with Claude, catching inconsistencies instantly and fixing them. Traditional PM workflows would have taken weeks for this cycle. I did it in days.

The PM Decisions That Mattered
  • Observation, not reaction: I didn't wait for complaints. I watched him use v1, noticed he was asking about moons, and proactively built the feature. This taught me the difference between reactive PM (waiting for feedback) and proactive PM (anticipating needs).
  • Consistency as a constraint: When I added the Moon Explorer tab, I realized every tab needed the same visual language, sorting logic, and clarity. That constraint forced me to standardize object rendering across Compare, Browse, and Moon Explorer. It made the entire product stronger, not just the new feature.
  • Sorting and organization matter: I added size sorting for the Browse tab, orbital order for planets, grouping by parent planet for moons. These weren't afterthoughts. They emerged from understanding how my son naturally explored the data.
What I Would Do Differently

For v1, I skipped formal discovery. No vision doc, no PRD, no conversation about requirements. It happened to work out because my son loved the core idea. But I got lucky. If I were starting over, I'd do light discovery upfront, even just asking questions: "What do you want to explore?" Then ship small, observe, and iterate based on what I see, not just what I hear. The difference between proactive observation and reactive feedback is huge, and documenting those observations early would have made the v1 to v2 transition even smoother.

What This Taught Me About Builder PM

This project crystallized something critical: builder PM is about more than just not needing to code. It's about the velocity to observe, hypothesize, and validate. In traditional PM, iterating on UX might take weeks. With Claude, it took days. That speed is a superpower if paired with good judgment. Watch your users. Notice what they need before they ask. Build proactively. Then refine relentlessly. That's builder PM in practice: observation plus velocity equals better products, faster.

Job Search Agent

Active

The problem: I'm searching for my next role. Rather than manually scouring job boards, I built an AI agent that finds promising opportunities, scores them against my criteria, and generates tailored resumes and cover letters. The agent runs on a schedule, sends me curated reports, and tracks my pipeline. In other words: a personal job search operator that runs 24/7.

✨ View on GitHub
What I Built
  • Autonomous agent that searches Indeed daily for matching roles
  • Multi-dimensional role fit scoring (Experience, Title/Seniority, Geography, Salary, AI Signal, Recruiter Attention)
  • Scheduled email digests with top 5 opportunities and detailed fit analysis
  • Google Sheets pipeline tracker that logs 13 data points per role
  • Resume and cover letter generator that tailors to job descriptions while preserving my voice
  • Operating Manual (MD file) that encodes my job targeting criteria, resume rules, and decision framework
How I Built It and What Surprised Me

I created a detailed operating manual in Markdown that became the agent's decision engine. Then I configured a Claude Cowork project with scheduled tasks (Monday/Wednesday/Friday job pulls, weekly pipeline reviews) connected to Indeed, Gmail, and Google Drive MCPs. What surprised me most was how straightforward it was to orchestrate multiple systems with clear instructions. The agent's outputs were surprisingly high quality. On the flip side, I hit some MCP limitations (Gmail couldn't auto-send, Google Sheets couldn't auto-update existing tables), but these are being addressed. The key insight: the quality of the agent is almost entirely determined by the quality of the operating manual.

The PM Decisions That Mattered
  • Operating Manual as the agent: Investing time in a detailed MD file with targeting criteria, honest gaps, resume rules, and scoring frameworks paid compound returns on every downstream output.
  • Scope ruthlessly: The problem wasn't find a job, it was find the right job. This meant limiting the search to my highest-fit opportunities and crafting tailored materials, not spraying resumes everywhere.
  • Define success metrics: An AI agent isn't set it and forget it. I need clear metrics to evaluate whether it's working, then iterate on the operating manual to keep it aligned.
What I Would Do Differently

Since this is a living system, I'd want to evolve it based on what I learn. Right now the scheduled digests and pipeline tracking are defined but not fully automated. As the MCP limitations lift and as I refine my targeting criteria based on real feedback from applications, I'll iterate the operating manual to keep the agent sharp.

What This Taught Me About Builder PM

Agentic AI is achievable for non-technical builders, and with the right direction, it can dramatically amplify output. I'm getting high-quality leads, fit analysis, tailored resumes, and cover letters in 10 minutes that would take me 100+ minutes manually. But the responsibility doesn't disappear. Like a manager, I need to hold the agent accountable to success metrics and iterate when it drifts. That's builder PM for agents: clear direction, measurable outcomes, and relentless iteration.
